The Tidal Effect: Unraveling the Influence on Wave Heights

The movements of the Earth, Moon, and Sun don't just tell time—they also shape the sea. Tides are like slow-moving waves around our planet, created by the gravitational pull between these celestial bodies. These tides affect the size and shape of waves that crash onto our shores, which is especially important to surfers.

Tides can make waves change dramatically. When the tide is high, waves can grow larger and reach further up the beach. When it's low, they break closer to shore and can be more powerful. Understanding how tides and waves interact helps us predict where and when the best waves for surfing will be.

Our analysis of tides and wave heights shows a pattern that can help us in many ways, from designing safer coastlines to planning the perfect time for surfing. Knowing how the tides affect waves helps us enjoy the ocean safely and fully.

Understanding Swell Direction and Wave Height

Wave directions are important for understanding where good surfing waves will come from. The winds over the ocean, sometimes from distant storms, create these swells. Once they hit the shore, they turn into waves for surfing.

Our chart shows which way the waves come from around Morro Bay. Different colors on the chart show different wave heights—the warmer the color, the bigger the wave.

You can also see Morro Rock on the map, a big rock that changes the way waves move. It seems that waves from the south get bigger at Morro Bay because of the way the sea floor and coast are shaped there.

Knowing where waves come from and how big they might be is really useful for things like surfing, sailing, and building on the coast. This chart helps people who love the sea make smart choices.

What is a Wave Period?

A wave period is how long it takes for one wave to follow another at a certain point. This helps us know how much energy a wave has. Bigger, well-shaped waves that surfers like have longer periods because they come from far away, often from big storms out at sea.

The diagram shows that waves with longer periods have a wider gap between them and bring more energy, making the waves higher when they reach the beach.

On the other hand, waves with shorter periods start closer to the shore, have less energy, and are smaller and weaker. They happen more often but aren't as good for surfing.

Looking at the wave period together with the wave heights on the graph, we can understand better how waves will affect the coast. This is really important for surfing, boating, and planning things along the shoreline.

Understanding Wave Partitions

Waves in the ocean come from different systems, and 'wave partitions' help us break down and study these systems separately. Each partition shows us a part of the bigger picture, including where the waves are coming from, how often they come, and how big they are.

We track six main wave types, each labeled from lotusSighPart0_mt to lotusSighPart5_mt. These labels help us measure and add up the heights of waves from each system to understand the total wave activity in the ocean.

When we talk about the 'total significant wave height,' it's the average height of the biggest waves out in the open ocean, which tells us about the energy of the waves. This is different from the 'breaking wave height,' which is the waves we see breaking at the beach.

Studying these different wave parts gives us a clearer idea of how complex the ocean's behavior is. This knowledge is very useful for things like guiding ships, managing offshore projects, and predicting the best times for surfing.

Weighted Sum of Wave Partitions vs. Total Significant Wave Height

This chart shows how different wave types combine to give us the average height of the biggest waves in the open sea, a number we call lotusSigh_mt. We use different weights, like [1, 0.6, 0.5, 0.4, 0.3, 0.2], for each wave type because some waves have a bigger impact than others.

While these numbers usually match up, they're not always perfect. That's because the ocean's waves are complex, and how we measure them can vary.

The term lotusSigh_mt is about the average size of the largest waves way out at sea, not the ones breaking at the beach. It's important for understanding overall sea conditions, helping ships to navigate safely, and predicting what the ocean will do.

What We Learn from Wave Patterns

Looking at a week's worth of hourly wave data, we can spot some trends:

  • The biggest waves happen mostly in the winter, from December to January, likely because of winter storms.
  • The smallest waves come in the calmer months of July to November.
  • The really big waves usually come from the west, which makes sense since Morro Bay faces the Pacific Ocean.
  • Tides don't seem to change the wave heights much during these times; there are other more important factors at play.
  • The time between waves has a small link to wave height, with bigger waves tending to follow one another more slowly.

This all shows us just how complicated the ocean is and all the things that affect the waves at Morro Bay.
